I — The Story They Told You

They said AI was invented.
They were wrong.

The official story goes like this: artificial intelligence appeared sometime after World War II, born fully formed in white rooms filled with humming machines and men in lab coats. It is Western. It is new. It belongs to Silicon Valley, to Stanford, to the future.

That story is a myth. And myths are not accidents. They are choices — choices about who gets to be remembered, who gets to feel at home in the future, and who gets to arrive as a stranger.

"They erased the names but not the work. They buried the lineage but not the logic."

The mathematics of decision-making under uncertainty — the skeleton of every AI system alive today — was formalized by David Blackwell, a Black mathematician who was denied membership to the Institute for Mathematical Statistics because of his race. The spatial intelligence behind GPS, the system that gives machines their sense of place in the world, was built by Gladys West, a Black woman working quietly at a naval base in Virginia.

They are not exceptions. They are proof. And their story points to something older and deeper than any institution.

II — The Deeper Root

Before code, there was
pattern.

Long before silicon, African civilizations were already solving the same problems artificial intelligence attempts to solve today. How do you choose when the outcome is uncertain? How do you act without full information? How do you encode memory so it can be retrieved across generations?

The Ifa divination system of the Yoruba — sixteen primary signs unfolding into 256 possible states, each associated with narratives, probabilities, ethical constraints, and prescribed actions — is a decision tree. It is a probabilistic reasoning system. It does not claim omniscience. It assumes uncertainty and offers structured guidance. That is the same problem every AI system attempts to solve.

The Lukasa memory boards of central Africa were external cognition — physical interfaces for encoding and retrieving vast amounts of historical, social, and procedural knowledge nonlinearly. Modern AI researchers call this embodied cognition and external memory systems. Africans were doing it centuries ago.

"Intelligence in these traditions was measured by alignment, timing, memory, and restraint — not speed, not domination."

This is not folklore. This is not coincidence. This is lineage. And it lives in you.
